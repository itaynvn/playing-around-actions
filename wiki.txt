<p><i><b>G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic book</a> that was published by <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> from 1982 to 1994. Based on <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a>'s <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero">G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</a> line of military-themed toys, the series has been credited for making <i>G.I. Joe</i> into a pop-culture phenomenon.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <i>G.I. Joe</i> was also the first comic book to be advertised on television, in what has been called a "historically crucial moment in media convergence".<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The series was written for most of its 155-issue run by comic book writer, artist, and editor <a href="/wiki/Larry_Hama" title="Larry Hama">Larry Hama</a>, and was notable for its realistic, character-based storytelling style, unusual for a toy comic at the time. Hama wrote the series spontaneously, never knowing how a story would end until it was finished, but worked closely with the artists, giving them sketches of the characters and major scenes. While most stories involved the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_Team" title="G.I. Joe Team">G.I. Joe Team</a> battling against the forces of <a href="/wiki/Cobra_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Cobra Command">Cobra Command</a>, an evil terrorist organization, many also focused on the relationships and <a href="/wiki/Back-story" class="mw-redirect" title="Back-story">background stories</a> of the characters. Hama created most characters in collaboration with Hasbro, and used a system of <a href="/wiki/File_card" class="mw-redirect" title="File card">file cards</a> to keep track of the personalities and fictional histories of his characters, which later became a major selling point for the action figure line.
</p><p><i>G.I. Joe</i> was Marvel's top-selling subscription title in 1985, and was receiving 1200 fan letters per week by 1987. The series has been credited with bringing in a new generation of comic book readers, since many children were introduced to the comic book medium through <i>G.I. Joe</i>, and later went on to read other comics.<sup id="cite_ref-Shooter2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shooter2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The comic book has been re-printed several times, and also translated in multiple languages. In addition to direct spin-offs of the comic book, several <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Devil%27s_Due)" class="mw-redirect" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero (Devil&#39;s Due)">revivals</a> and <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_(IDW_Publishing)" title="G.I. Joe (IDW Publishing)">reboots</a> have been published throughout the 2000s.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publication history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a> noted the success of <a href="/wiki/Kenner_Products" title="Kenner Products">Kenner Products</a>' <a href="/wiki/Kenner_Star_Wars_action_figures" title="Kenner Star Wars action figures">Star Wars action figures</a>, and decided to re-launch their long-running <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe" title="G.I. Joe">G.I. Joe</a></i> property as <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero">G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</a></i> with 3.75 inches (9.5&#160;cm) scale <a href="/wiki/Action_figure" title="Action figure">action figures</a> rather than the traditional 12 inches (30&#160;cm) scale. Hasbro also decided that they wanted the new figures to have a back story.<sup id="cite_ref-MetropolisComics_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetropolisComics-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1981, Hasbro CEO <a href="/wiki/Stephen_D._Hassenfeld" title="Stephen D. Hassenfeld">Stephen Hassenfeld</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> President <a href="/wiki/Jim_Galton" title="Jim Galton">Jim Galton</a> met by coincidence at a charity fundraiser and Hassenfeld shared Hasbro's plans for the G.I. Joe relaunch. Galton offered Marvel's services as creative consultants, and Hassenfeld agreed to allow Marvel attempt to design a concept for G.I. Joe.<sup id="cite_ref-Shooter1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shooter1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coincidentally Larry Hama, then an editor at Marvel, had begun to design characters and background for a series concept he was pitching that would be entitled <i>Fury Force</i>, about a team of futuristic super-soldiers affiliated with <a href="/wiki/S.H.I.E.L.D." title="S.H.I.E.L.D.">S.H.I.E.L.D.</a>, an existing <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a> property combining <a href="/wiki/Spy-fi_(subgenre)#Military_SF" title="Spy-fi (subgenre)">military and science fiction genre elements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MetropolisComics_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetropolisComics-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Legends_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legends-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As Hama tells it, he got the job of writing for the series because Marvel had asked every other available creator to write it and no one else would.<sup id="cite_ref-Truitt_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Truitt-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Unable to find other writing work, he later said that, "if they had asked me to write Barbie, I would have done that, too".<sup id="cite_ref-CBR_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Soon after this, Hasbro hosted a meeting with Hama, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Shooter" title="Jim Shooter">Jim Shooter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_DeFalco" title="Tom DeFalco">Tom DeFalco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archie_Goodwin_(comics)" title="Archie Goodwin (comics)">Archie Goodwin</a>, and Nelson Yomtov to discuss the future of the property. It was at this meeting that Goodwin suggested the idea of <a href="/wiki/Cobra_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Cobra Command">Cobra Command</a> as a recurring enemy for G.I. Joe to fight (similar to the <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(Marvel_Comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydra (Marvel Comics)">HYDRA</a> terrorist organization - recurring enemies to the aforementioned S.H.I.E.L.D. organization). Prior to this, Hasbro had not considered giving G.I. Joe an enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Based on the results of this meeting, Hasbro contracted Marvel to produce a comic book series featuring the toys.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first issue was published in June 1982, containing two stories, both of which were written by Hama. The first story, "Operation: Lady Doomsday", was drawn by <a href="/wiki/Herb_Trimpe" title="Herb Trimpe">Herb Trimpe</a>, who drew most of the early issues and also wrote issue #9, and the second story, "Hot Potato", was drawn by <a href="/wiki/Don_Perlin" title="Don Perlin">Don Perlin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This issue introduced many basic concepts of the G.I. Joe universe, such as the Joes having a base under a motor pool, and introduced the iconic "original 13" <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_Team" title="G.I. Joe Team">G.I. Joe Team</a> members. The issue also introduced two recurring villains, <a href="/wiki/Cobra_Commander" title="Cobra Commander">Cobra Commander</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baroness_(G.I._Joe)" title="Baroness (G.I. Joe)">Baroness</a>. Whereas Cobra Commander and the various Joes already had action figures issued, The Baroness is the earliest example of a <i>G.I. Joe</i> character whose first appearance in the comics predated the conception of their action figure.<sup id="cite_ref-CBR_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Irving_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the early stories were completed in one issue, but multi-part stories began to appear by the middle of the series' first year of publication,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and there were hints of the ongoing storylines that would later characterize the series. In May 1983, issue #11 introduced many new characters, including most of the 1983 action figure line and the villain <a href="/wiki/Destro" title="Destro">Destro</a>, who would become a frequently recurring character. Many subsequent storylines involved the machinations and power struggles between him, Cobra Commander, and the Baroness. Issue #11 established a pattern for the series in which every so often Marvel would publish an issue introducing a group of characters and vehicles that represented the new year's toy offerings.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An early highlight was 1984's "Snake Eyes: The Origin" Parts I &amp; II, published in issues #26-27. This issue established <a href="/wiki/Snake_Eyes_(G.I._Joe)" title="Snake Eyes (G.I. Joe)">Snake Eyes'</a> complicated background, and tied his character into many other characters, both G.I. Joe and Cobra. Hama considers it to be his favorite storyline from the Marvel run.<sup id="cite_ref-JoeBattlelines2006_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JoeBattlelines2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 1986, echoing events portrayed in the TV series, <i>G.I. Joe</i> #49 was published, introducing the character of <a href="/wiki/Serpentor" title="Serpentor">Serpentor</a>, a genetically created amalgam of history's greatest warriors. Serpentor played a significant role in the Cobra Civil War, which occurred in issues #73-76, a landmark story event that involved nearly every G.I. Joe and Cobra character vying for control of <a href="/wiki/Cobra_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Cobra Island">Cobra Island</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years_and_cancellation">Later years and cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years and cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <i>G.I. Joe</i> began, most toy tie-in comics lasted an average of two years,<sup id="cite_ref-Yojoe97_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yojoe97-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> so G.I. Joe, lasting for 12 years, was considered a runaway success. Through the years, the comic book series chronicled the adventures of G.I. Joe and Cobra, using a consistent storyline.<sup id="cite_ref-30thAnnPg159_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30thAnnPg159-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In the early 1990s, however, it began to drop in quality, and was canceled by Marvel in 1994 with issue #155 due to low sales.<sup id="cite_ref-Yojoe97_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yojoe97-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Hasbro canceled the <i>A Real American Hero</i> toy line in the same year. Between the lack of new toys and the cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(1989_TV_series)" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero (1989 TV series)">second TV series</a> three years earlier, the comic book could not count on the same cross-platform support it had enjoyed in the past. The target demographic had also changed considerably. According to Hama:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It reached the end of its half-life. Until G.I. Joe and Transformers, toy books had a life expectancy of 1-2 years – 3 years was considered a long time. Hasbro didn’t expect the toy-line to have that much life in it. Also, the market had changed completely. When I first started doing store signings, there were lines around the block and it was all 10-year-old boys. The last time I did a story signing in New York City, everybody was over 30, and two of the guys who showed up were mailmen who had skipped off their routes to get their books signed.<sup id="cite_ref-Generalissimo_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generalissimo-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The final issue featured a stand-alone story titled "A Letter from Snake Eyes". Narrating from his perspective, Snake Eyes tells his story through recollections of his many comrades-in-arms who have died over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shortly after the final issue, <i>G.I. Joe Special</i> #1 was released in February 1995, containing alternate art for issue #61 by <a href="/wiki/Todd_McFarlane" title="Todd McFarlane">Todd McFarlane</a>. McFarlane was the original penciller for issue #61, but his artwork had been rejected by Larry Hama as unacceptable, and so <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Rogers" title="Marshall Rogers">Marshall Rogers</a> was brought in to pencil the final published version. In the years following, McFarlane became a superstar comic artist, and Marvel eventually decided to print the unpublished work.<sup id="cite_ref-CBR182_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBR182-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hasbro used television advertising to publicize the series, and when the first one aired in 1982 featuring <i>G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</i> #1, it was the first time a commercial had ever been used to promote a comic book.<sup id="cite_ref-BSC_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSC-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Since the commercials were technically promoting the comic books rather than the toys, they allowed Hasbro to circumvent television regulations mandating that toy commercials could not contain more than ten seconds of animation.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> By not showcasing any characters and toys outside of the comic book context, they were able to include a full thirty seconds of animation.<sup id="cite_ref-Generalissimo_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generalissimo-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Marvel was paid $5 million by Hasbro to produce the commercials through its animation division <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Productions" title="Marvel Productions">Marvel Productions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Larry Hama relates the genesis of the commercials:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There were only a few seconds of animation you could have in a toy commercial, and you had to show the toy, so people wouldn’t get totally deluded. Somebody at Hasbro (who was actually sort of a genius) named Bob Pruprish, realized that a comic book was protected under the first amendment, and there couldn't be restrictions based on how you advertised for a publication.<sup id="cite_ref-Irving_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>
Between the toy line, comic books, commercials and subsequent cartoon series, Hasbro's marketing plan was highly successful and eventually became an industry standard, an early example of a practice that would years later be described by Jenkins as a "transmedia narrative".<sup id="cite_ref-jenkins_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenkins-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-continuum_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-continuum-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Although the adolescent male demographic was the traditional comic book reader, an unintended result of the TV advertising tie-ins was that they attracted people who were not traditional comic book readers. In an interview, Hama stated: <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I think [the commercials have] also opened it up to a very different type of audience. I get a lot of letters from girls. I get a lot of letters from young housewives who sort of started watching the cartoons with their kids and sort of started getting into the characters, and then somewhere along the line they picked up the comic book and they started following the stories and got caught up in the continuity.<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The comic book's popularity with women has also been attributed to the strong female characters featured in the comic, such as <a href="/wiki/Scarlett_(G.I._Joe)" title="Scarlett (G.I. Joe)">Scarlett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Jaye_(G.I._Joe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Jaye (G.I. Joe)">Lady Jaye</a>. Since very few of the G.I. Joe action figures were female, Hama tended to frequently use all of the female characters, including those that were created as recurring characters in the comics.<sup id="cite_ref-JoeBattlelines2006_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JoeBattlelines2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_style">Writing style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A smiling, middle-aged man with thin dark hair sitting at a red table wearing a black T-shirt." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg/120px-LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg" decoding="async" width="120" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg/180px-LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg/240px-LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LarryHama5.23.09ByLuigiNovi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Larry Hama, creator and writer of <i>G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</i></div></div></div>
<p>Many readers praised the series for its attention to detail and realism in the area of military tactics and procedures. Much of this was due to Hama's military experience (he was drafted into the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers during the Vietnam war), but he also did a large amount of research in order to be as up-to-date as possible. He frequented Sky Books, a military-oriented bookstore in New York, and read many <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Field_Manuals" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army Field Manuals">U.S. Army field manuals</a> and technical manuals, and also credits his friend Lee Russel, a military historian, for helping him with research.<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
In style and plot structure, the comic often made use of overlapping story threads. According to Hama: <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We’ve been following one basic storyline pretty much in the comic for fifty issues. It’s sort of like an extended soap opera, although I try to have a real solid resolution at the end of each book. But I like to keep some plot threads going. There's a sort of episodic quality to some of the earlier books, like one episode will last six issues. That will resolve completely, but two issues into it another thread may have started. At any given time there’s probably about three overlapping threads.<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>
The story arcs coincided with each of the new lines of G.I. Joe toys that Hasbro was producing, however the comic book was not directly influenced by the toy products. Of the Hasbro/Marvel relationship, Hama observed that the toy company did not demand the book to be written a certain way: <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We're the final word on what happens with the book. Hasbro has been extremely open about it, [and] I don't write this as a kiddie book. I don't write <i>G.I. Joe</i> any differently from the way I write <i>Wolverine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30thAnnPg159_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30thAnnPg159-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Hama wrote out page-by-page plots for all of the issues he wrote, with most pages having four to six panels. He worked very closely with the artists in plotting the book,<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and wrote the series spontaneously, never knowing how an issue would end until he got to the last page.<sup id="cite_ref-CBR_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization">Characterization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Characterization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_Team" title="G.I. Joe Team">G.I. Joe Team</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_characters" title="List of G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero characters">List of G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero characters</a></div>
<p>In the first issue, it is revealed that the team’s official moniker is <b>Special Counter-Terrorist Unit Delta</b>. <i>G.I. Joe</i> featured an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a>, with the original thirteen characters being <a href="/wiki/Hawk_(G.I._Joe)" title="Hawk (G.I. Joe)">Hawk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stalker_(G.I._Joe)" title="Stalker (G.I. Joe)">Stalker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breaker_(G.I._Joe)" title="Breaker (G.I. Joe)">Breaker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clutch_(G.I._Joe)" title="Clutch (G.I. Joe)">Clutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scarlett_(G.I._Joe)" title="Scarlett (G.I. Joe)">Scarlett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake_Eyes_(G.I._Joe)" title="Snake Eyes (G.I. Joe)">Snake Eyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_N%27_Roll_(G.I._Joe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock N&#39; Roll (G.I. Joe)">Rock ‘n Roll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steeler_(G.I._Joe)" title="Steeler (G.I. Joe)">Steeler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_(G.I._Joe)" title="Grand Slam (G.I. Joe)">Grand Slam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flash_(G.I._Joe)" title="Flash (G.I. Joe)">Flash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short-Fuze_(G.I._Joe)" title="Short-Fuze (G.I. Joe)">Short-Fuze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grunt_(G.I._Joe)" title="Grunt (G.I. Joe)">Grunt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zap_(G.I._Joe)" title="Zap (G.I. Joe)">Zap</a>. This reflected their origins in the Hasbro toy line, with the initial characters being the same as the action figures in the original 1982 release of the toy line.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The team roster expanded as additional action figures were released.
</p><p>Hama created most characters in collaboration with Hasbro. Hasbro would send him character sketches and brief descriptions of each character's military specialties, and Hama would create detailed dossiers on each characters, giving them distinct personalities and <a href="/wiki/Back-story" class="mw-redirect" title="Back-story">background stories</a>. Hama kept track of each character on <a href="/wiki/File_card" class="mw-redirect" title="File card">file cards</a>, and eventually Hasbro decided to reprint shortened versions of these dossiers as file cards on the packaging for the action figures.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The file cards themselves became a selling point for the toy line, and appealed to both children and adults. Hama noted that:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... it has to be read on two levels ... A ten-year-old kid has to be able to read it and think it's absolutely straight [but] there should be a joke in there for the adult. One of the factors that helped sell G.I. Joe [figures] was that the salesmen who sold it to retailers used the dossiers as a selling point.<sup id="cite_ref-Irving_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Hama tended to base the personalities of the characters on people that he knew, and he credits this technique for the realism of his characterization.<sup id="cite_ref-CBR_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He later said that "events and continuity never meant anything to me. The important thing was the characters".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initially, the response to the new comic book was muted due to its low status as a "toy book". However its popularity eventually grew, and is credited with bringing in a new generation of comic readers.<sup id="cite_ref-Generalissimo_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generalissimo-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Within two months of the toy line's launch, "some 20 per cent of boys from ages five to twelve had two or more G.I. Joe toys",<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and by 1988/89 a survey conducted by Hasbro found that "two out of every three boys between the ages of five and eleven owned at least one <i>G.I. Joe</i> figure".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> These boys were drawn to the <i>G.I. Joe</i> comic book through its association with the toy line, and then went on to other comics.<sup id="cite_ref-Shooter2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shooter2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As Hama puts it:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It was a toy book. Very uncool to the fan-boys at the time. It never got reviewed in the fan press. Totally ignored. The kids who bought G.I. Joe were a totally new crowd who were coming into the comic shops for the first time because they had the toys and they saw the commercials. Many of them started to buy other comics while they were there.<sup id="cite_ref-Generalissimo_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generalissimo-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>According to comic book historian <a href="/wiki/John_Jackson_Miller" title="John Jackson Miller">John Jackson Miller</a>, Hasbro's promotion of the comic resulted in dramatic sales increases, from 157,920 copies per month in 1983 to 331,475 copies per month in 1985, making it one of Marvel's "strongest titles".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> According to Jim Shooter, G.I. Joe was Marvel's number one subscription title in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-Shooter1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shooter1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By 1987, the main title was getting 1200 letters every week.<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Hama read every one, and sent out fifty to one hundred hand-written replies every week.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Interlude.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Page form a comic book depicting a ninja warrior on a small aircraft flying towards an ominous mountain castle, with the words &quot;Silent Interlude&quot; in the foreground." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Silent_Interlude.jpg/120px-Silent_Interlude.jpg" decoding="async" width="120" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Silent_Interlude.jpg/180px-Silent_Interlude.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2f/Silent_Interlude.jpg 2x" data-file-width="212" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Interlude.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Silent Interlude" from <i>G.I. Joe</i> #21 (March 1984). Art by Larry Hama and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Leialoha" title="Steve Leialoha">Steve Leialoha</a>.</div></div></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1023981488">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .rquote{width:auto!important;float:none!important}}</style><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote rquote" style="float: right; width: 33%;"><p>In comics, where images are at least half the language, pictures can form valid narrative as easily and functionally as words – and, at times, without them ... such as the lauded GI. Joe&#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; "Silent Interlude" #21. It does at least prove, as if there were any doubt, the viability of visual narrative.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/A._David_Lewis" title="A. David Lewis">A. David Lewis</a>, <i>The shape of comic book reading</i><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>
<p>An early issue that attracted much attention, both positive and negative, was <i>G.I. Joe</i> #21, titled "Silent Interlude", which was told entirely without words or sound effects. In a 1987 interview, Hama explained that the motivation for the story was that he "... wanted to see if [he] could do a story that was a real, complete story - beginning, middle, end, conflict, characterization, action, solid resolution—without balloons or captions or sound effects".<sup id="cite_ref-ComicsInterview_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComicsInterview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-enjoy-silence_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enjoy-silence-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> At first, the issue was controversial; some readers felt cheated that it had no words and could be "read" so quickly,<sup id="cite_ref-JoeBattlelines2006_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JoeBattlelines2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but it eventually became one of the series' most enduring and influential issues.<sup id="cite_ref-comixology_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-comixology-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Issue #21 has been recognized as a modern comic classic,<sup id="cite_ref-Legends_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legends-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and has become a prime example of comics' visual storytelling power. Comic book artist and theorist <a href="/wiki/Scott_McCloud" title="Scott McCloud">Scott McCloud</a> (author of <i><a href="/wiki/Understanding_Comics" title="Understanding Comics">Understanding Comics</a></i>) describes "Silent Interlude" as "... a kind of watershed moment for cartoonists of [our] generation. Everyone remembers it. All these things came out of it. It was like 9/11".<sup id="cite_ref-comixology_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-comixology-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The issue would eventually be ranked #44 in <a href="/wiki/Wizard_(magazine)" title="Wizard (magazine)">Wizard Magazine's</a> listing of the "100 Best Single Issues Since You Were Born",<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and #6 on <a href="/wiki/Io9" title="Io9">io9</a>'s "10 Issues of Ongoing Comics that Prove Single Issues Can Be Great".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-offs">Spin-offs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spin-offs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1985, <i>G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</i> spawned an <a href="/wiki/Annual_publication" title="Annual publication">annual publication</a> called <i>G.I. Joe: Yearbook.</i> <i>G.I. Joe: Yearbook</i> differed from the typical comic book annual publication in that it was more like a magazine. Each issue contained articles about the animated TV program, a summary of the comic book's plot to date, and one or two original stories written by Larry Hama. <i>G.I. Joe: Yearbook</i> ran until 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, the success of <i>A Real American Hero</i> lead Marvel to produce a second title: <i>G.I. Joe: Special Missions</i> which lasted 28 issues.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Herb Trimpe was the artist for most of the run, with <a href="/wiki/Dave_Cockrum" title="Dave Cockrum">Dave Cockrum</a> providing pencils on several issues. Spinning out of <i>G.I. Joe</i> #50 and set in the same continuity,<sup id="cite_ref-Weiner_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> the series presented more intense violence and a more ambiguous morality than the main title, and the Joes faced enemies who were not related to Cobra.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Each issue usually featured a stand-alone mission focusing on a small group of Joes.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two mini-series were also produced. The first, <i>G.I. Joe: Order of Battle</i>, was a 4-issue mini-series running from December 1986 to March 1987, reprinting the data found on the action figures' <a href="/wiki/File_card" class="mw-redirect" title="File card">file cards</a> with some edits. Written by Hama, with all-new artwork by Trimpe, the first two issues featured G.I. Joe members, while the third issue focused on the members of <a href="/wiki/Cobra_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Cobra Command">Cobra Command</a>, and the fourth highlighted the vehicles and equipment used by both organizations. A <a href="/wiki/Trade_paperback_(comics)" title="Trade paperback (comics)">trade-paperback</a> edition, which included material from all four issues, was published in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The second, <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_and_the_Transformers" title="G.I. Joe and the Transformers">G.I. Joe and the Transformers</a></i>, was a 4-issue mini-series running from January to April 1987. The story had the Joes and the <a href="/wiki/Autobots" class="mw-redirect" title="Autobots">Autobots</a> joining forces to stop the <a href="/wiki/Decepticons" class="mw-redirect" title="Decepticons">Decepticons</a> and Cobra from destroying the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiner_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> A trade paperback later collected all four issues.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reprints_and_revivals">Reprints and revivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Marvel_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reprints and revivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe_(comics)" title="G.I. Joe (comics)"><i>G.I. Joe</i> (comics)</a></div>
<p>The first 37 issues of the main series were released in thirteen <a href="/wiki/Digest_size" title="Digest size">digests</a> titled <i>G.I. Joe Comic Magazine</i>. Subsequently, <i>Tales of G.I. Joe</i> reprinted the first fifteen issues of <i>G.I. Joe</i> on a higher quality paper stock than that used for the main comic. Four <i>Yearbooks</i> (1985–1988) also collected some previous stories, summarized events, and published new stories that tied into the main title, aside from the first <i>Yearbook</i>, which re-printed the seminal first issue. The series was also translated into several languages, including <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_French" title="Canadian French">French (Canada)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>G.I. Joe European Missions</i> was published monthly from June 1988 until August 1989. The European Missions series are all reprints of <i>Action Force Monthly</i>, which was published in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Action_Force" title="Action Force">Action Force</a></i> series, these were all original stories, never before seen in the U.S. They were not written by Larry Hama.
</p><p>In July 2001, <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Due_Publishing" title="Devil&#39;s Due Publishing">Devil's Due Publishing</a> acquired the rights to <i>G.I. Joe</i> and began publication of <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(Devil%27s_Due)" class="mw-redirect" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero (Devil&#39;s Due)">G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero Vol. 2</a></i>. Originally intended to be a four-issue limited series, strong sales led to it being upgraded to ongoing, and it lasted for 43 issues, before being relaunched as a new series <i><a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_America%27s_Elite" title="G.I. Joe: America&#39;s Elite">G.I. Joe: America's Elite</a></i>, which lasted for 36 issues. Devil's Due's license with Hasbro expired in 2008 and was not renewed.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Building on the success of the Devil's Due Comics run of <i>G.I. Joe</i>, Marvel Comics collected the first 50 issues in five <a href="/wiki/Trade_paperback_(comics)" title="Trade paperback (comics)">trade paperbacks</a>, with ten issues in each book. The trade paperbacks each featured a new cover artwork drawn by J. Scott Campbell.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiner_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/IDW_Publishing" title="IDW Publishing">IDW Publishing</a> began to re-publish the original series again as <i>Classic G.I. Joe</i>, and like Marvel before it, collects ten issues in each volume; the last few collections have slightly more issues in order to conclude with the 15th paperback volume, which was published in August, 2012. Other collections for the spin-off <i>Special Missions</i> and assorted Annuals also appeared. In November 2012, IDW restarted their reprint series with the hardcover <i>G.I. JOE: The Complete Collection, Vol. 1</i>, intending to reprint all the stories in reading order.
</p><p>IDW also revived the original <i>G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero</i> continuity, as <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe:_A_Real_American_Hero_(IDW_Publishing)" title="G.I. Joe: A Real American Hero (IDW Publishing)">an ongoing series</a> in 2010, with a special #155½ issue released on <a href="/wiki/Free_Comic_Book_Day" title="Free Comic Book Day">Free Comic Book Day</a>. Following with issue #156 onwards in July 2010 with every issue in the series written by Larry Hama, this lasted until December 2022, concluding with issue 300.<sup id="cite_ref-Truitt_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Truitt-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
